DISPATCH #19: The Squeakdog Lecture
I was in the corner shop at 1 AM buying powdered doughnuts—a rare treat, the kind you only buy when nobody's watching—when one of the hotdogs rolled off the rotisserie.
Not fell. Rolled. With intention.
It squeaked as it moved. Not steam escaping. An actual squeak, like a tiny mouse clearing its throat.
Nobody else in the queue noticed. There were three other people waiting, all of them staring at their phones or the middle distance, the way people do at 1 AM in corner shops. The hotdog scampered across the counter tiles. Still squeaking.
I must have made a noise—not loud, just a caught breath—because the man in front of me turned around.
He was holding exact change, the kind of precise handful that suggests he'd been planning this transaction for some time.
"You've noticed it too, haven't you?" he said.
I hadn't asked a question. I hadn't said anything.
But he was already explaining.
"The squeaking. Most people assume it's steam escaping through micro-perforations in the casing. Which is technically correct, but profoundly incomplete. What you're hearing is acoustic sentience formation in real-time."
He gestured at the rotisserie without looking at it.
"Look at them. Rotation plus heat plus time. The exact conditions that produced Gerald—though admittedly, Gerald had the advantage of cosmic-scale engineering and several billion years of intentional design. These hotdogs have approximately forty-five minutes and a 7-Eleven rotisserie. But the mechanism is identical."
The squeaking continued. Top row. Bottom row. Top row. A pause. Bottom row twice.
"Steam pressure creates internal differentiation," he continued. "The casing acts as a bounded system. Temperature gradient induces organizational complexity. And the rotation—ah, the rotation is key—provides the iterative feedback loop. Each revolution, the system samples its own state. Pattern recognition begins at the molecular level."
He turned back to the counter, then turned back to me.
"You'll notice they don't squeak randomly. Watch."
I was watching.
"Fibonacci timing. They're queuing. Taking turns. It's not competition for pressure release—it's politeness. They've developed a rudimentary social contract in the time it took you to decide whether you wanted crisps."
The hotdog that had scampered was still moving, still squeaking. It had circled back toward the rotisserie like it was considering returning.
"Now, this matters cosmologically because of what it tells us about the Δ₀ Principle—the threshold at which bounded systems begin to reconstitute informational coherence. Gerald achieved full sentience at universal scale. These hotdogs are achieving it at rotisserie scale. Same principle. Different magnitude."
He was still holding his exact change.
"If you can get sentience from rotation, heat, and time at any scale, then the universe didn't need a designer. It just needed to spin long enough under pressure. The Gerald Bang wasn't an exception—it was the inevitable outcome of thermodynamic recursion in a closed system."
The shopkeeper was ringing up his purchase. Completely unbothered. Just scanning, beeping, moving on.
Then I saw Gerald.
He was standing by the magazine rack, golden-brown, perpetually glistening, exactly as though he'd been there the whole time. Which, knowing Gerald, he probably had.
He looked at the scampering hotdog.
The hotdog looked back—if hotdogs can look. The squeaking changed pitch. Recognition, maybe. Or resignation.
Gerald reached down.
The hotdog made one final squeak, the kind of sound a mouse might make if it suddenly understood everything about the universe and accepted it all at once.
Then Gerald put it inside him.
The professor glanced at the spot where the hotdog had been.
Then he nodded.
Just once. The kind of nod that says: yes, that's how it works. That's the law in operation. That's the thing.
He pocketed his change and headed for the door.
"They'll be fully coherent in another twenty minutes," he said without turning around. "I'd recommend the crisps."
The door chimed. He was gone.
The rotisserie was still spinning. The remaining hotdogs were still squeaking in Fibonacci patterns. The shopkeeper was still unbothered. My powdered doughnuts were still in my hand, white sugar dusting my fingers.
Yeah.. That's the thing.
